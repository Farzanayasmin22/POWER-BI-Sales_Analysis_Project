A two-sample independent t-test was performed to compare the insurance charges of smokers and non-smokers.¶

The results showed a very high t-statistic (46.66) and an extremely small p-value (8.27 × 10⁻²⁸³), which is far below the 0.05 significance level. This indicates that the difference in average medical charges between smokers and non-smokers is statistically significant. Smokers pay much higher insurance charges than non-smokers, and this difference is not due to random chance. These findings strongly support the insights obtained from the boxplot, correlation heatmap, and regression model, confirming that smoking is the most influential factor in determining insurance costs

#Eigen decomposition
eigen_value , eigen_vec = np.linalg.eig(cov_matrix)

sorted_idx = np.argsort(eigen_value)[::-1]
sorted_eigval = eigen_value[sorted_idx]
sorted_eigvec = eigen_vec[:, sorted_idx]
Each principal component corresponds to one eigenvalue–eigenvector pair.¶

The largest eigenvalue represents the direction of maximum variance and its eigenvector becomes the first principal component (PC1). After sorting, the first column of the sorted eigenvector matrix corresponds to the largest eigenvalue, the second column corresponds to the second-largest eigenvalue, and so on. the largest variances occurred in ‘age’ and ‘BMI’, indicating that these variables have the widest spread among customers. Features such as children, smoker, sex, and region have relatively smaller variances because they are either discrete or binary dummy variables.

PC1 = eigenvector of largest eigenvalue

PC2 = eigenvector of second largest eigenvalue

PC3 = eigenvector of third largest eigenvalue
